\section*{Abstract}
RL agents are increasingly trained on third-party simulators, making the simulator a supply-chain trust anchor. DAZE shows that a malicious wrapper can implant reward-free backdoors by changing the learner-visible transition after an action is submitted, so action-level logging can miss the attack~\cite{daze}. Prior RL backdoor work mainly studies policy triggers or sanitization~\cite{trojdrl,backdoorl,badrl}; we protect the simulator data plane. \emph{Attested Transition Execution} (ATE) admits an update only when an approved measured step relation, exact replay, or enrolled receipt service vouches for the full transition record. On original DAZE Hopper/Reacher PPO, ATE restores clean-level ASR/return and admits zero tampered updates; SAC Reacher drops from ASR 0.9996 to 0.

\section*{Claim and Contributions}
\textbf{Claim.} For an approved measured closure with an untrusted wrapper, DAZE-style transition tampering cannot feed forged updates into training without failing ATE admission. \textbf{Contributions.} (i) We frame wrapper backdoor defense as transition attestation, not anomaly detection. (ii) Action-only logging is insufficient: Hopper termination tampering preserves $a_{\rm sub}=a_{\rm exec}$ yet reaches ASR 0.982. (iii) ATE restores original-DAZE Hopper/Reacher to clean-level ASR/return and admits zero poisoned updates over 3.94M checks.

\section*{Design}
\begin{center}
\includegraphics[width=0.78\linewidth]{ate_diagram.png}\\
\small \textbf{Figure 1.} ATE inserts a transition-admission gate between the wrapper and learner.
\end{center}
ATE checks learner-visible transitions rather than wrapper self-reports, accepting a transition only if the approved step relation validates:
\[
(s,o,a,T_\theta(a,s,t,\mathrm{rng}),s',o',r,d,\tau).
\]
Here $s,o$ denote state/observation; $a$ the submitted action; $T_\theta$ the measured executed action; $s',o'$ post-state/observation; $r$ reward; $d$ termination/truncation; and $\tau$ receipt metadata. $T_\theta$ covers approved actuator transforms; receipt mode follows measured-evidence principles~\cite{rats}.

\section*{Systems Evidence}
ATE also passed 168 MuJoCo promotion checks, 57 software-root protocol cases, 71 AMC-ATE stress cases, and 1.57M Brax/JAX GPU receipt records with zero accepted violations or false rejects. Code and result tables are public~\cite{atecode}.

\section*{Boundary and Deployment}
The approved closure covers simulator core, configuration/assets, RNG schedule, observation/termination code, reward inputs, and allowed actuator transforms; the wrapper is untrusted. ATE guarantees admission integrity for that closure, not safety of a maliciously approved closure. Current evidence targets exact CPU replay and software roots; remote clusters can bind receipts to TPM-style integrity verification~\cite{mujoco,riv}.
